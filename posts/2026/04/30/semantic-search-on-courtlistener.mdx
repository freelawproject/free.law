<p className="lead">Semantic search has arrived on CourtListener—you can now search case law using natural language, directly from the website.</p>

Last November, we [launched semantic search through our API][api-post], bringing a new way to find case law by meaning rather than exact keywords. The next step we're taking today is to make this powerful search available to everyone on the website—no API required.

Thousands of users place hundreds of thousands of searches on our website each day. We can't wait to see how this new feature enhances research on the [CourtListener][cl] platform!

## What Is Semantic Search?

<RightInfoBox border={true}>
  <h3 className="mt-2 mb-2">How It Works</h3>
  <p className="my-0">An encoder machine learning model, fine-tuned for information retrieval, represents legal opinions as embeddings (high-dimensional numbers) that capture their semantic meaning. When you submit a query, the same model translates it into embeddings, and an approximate nearest neighbor algorithm identifies opinions with the most semantically similar meanings.</p>
  <p className="my-0">For a detailed comparison of keyword versus semantic search, check out our help page on [Citegeist][cg].</p>
  <PurpleButton href="https://www.courtlistener.com/">Try It Now</PurpleButton>
</RightInfoBox>

Traditional keyword search requires that your query contain the exact same words as an opinion for it to turn up. Semantic search takes a different approach by leveraging the **meaning and intent** of your query. This surfaces relevant decisions even when they use different terminology than your query.

For example, a keyword search for "tenant evicted for having a dog" will only match opinions containing those exact words. A semantic search for the same query can also find cases about "landlord removed renter for violating pet policy"—because the meaning is the same.

[cg]: https://www.courtlistener.com/help/citegeist/

### Hybrid Search

Sometimes you need to combine the precision of keywords with the flexibility of natural language. With **hybrid search**, you can—just wrap your must-have terms in quotation marks.

For example: 

<blockquote className="not-prose border-l-4 border-gray-300 pl-4 py-2 my-4">"Fourth Amendment" unreasonable search of a student's phone at school</blockquote>

This ensures "Fourth Amendment" appears in every result, while the rest of the query is matched semantically.

### Filters Work Too

All your favorite filters—dates, courts, citation counts, and more—work seamlessly with semantic search, just like they do with keyword search.

## How to Use It

We've added a simple toggle to the search bar: click the **&** / **?** icon to switch between keyword and semantic search. Your last selection is saved, so you'll stay in the same mode when you return.

## Also Available via API

If you're building tools on top of CourtListener, semantic search has been available through our [Search API][api] since November 2025. Check out our [API announcement][api-post] for details on integration, hybrid search, and more.
